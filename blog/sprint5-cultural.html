Sprint 5 - Cultural

What are three new things you have learned about yourself and your ego as a result of the core learning?


I learned that it’s always good to reassess your views of your identity and values. In the past I’ve done quite a bit of self reflection to learn more about who I am as a person, but that was a while ago. Doing it this time, I found that some of my values had changed, not drastically, but it was important to note that I had changed.

I learnt a lot about how I work when not prompted by classes or bosses or managers to motivate me, it was all under my own schedule. When I used to study in the past, I found it hard to fine time to focus and study as I felt under pressure by deadlines and assignments. I found being more autonomous let me choose when I wanted to study and I felt more motivated because of it.

Lastly I found neuroplasticity really interesting. I find myself taking on hobbies quite often where I give up because I don’t have the motivation to keep practicing, and just assume I don’t have the talent each time I try. Knowing that there are actual connections being made in my brain when I practice gives me more reassurance and trust in the idea of constant practice.


What are the role of values, empathy and self awareness in learning and programming?


Values, empathy and self awareness are all important factors around emotional intelligence. Emotional intelligence is important to understand yourself and others. It's important to be an effective communicator, friend, colleague, student, teacher, programmer and so on. A good knowledge of these principles means that you can understand and manage emotions in an effective and positive way. This can helpm reduce stress and anxiety, improve you empathy for others, improve relationships and avoid and defuse conflicts.

What has surprised you the most about the core learning?


Definitely how much you learn about yourself. You always feel like you have a good idea of who are you as a person, but taking a good hard look always brings out new things. It’s like It’s also interesting to see how others think about you compared to yourself. I found that the values that I feel attached were not what others thought I’d put the highest, which was interesting. I reevaluated with this feedback to see if I felt they would change, but I found no change, so it was interesting to see how different others opinions and your own opinions of yourself are.

What were the most challenging aspects of the core learning?


Getting effective feedback consistently from my friends and family. It was difficult get feedback that was constructive. I tried wording questions in a way to make it easier for my peers to provide constructive feedback, but it wasn’t fully understood. What I got back was mostly rebuttals of my opinions but without any argument (just a “no, but I don’t know why I think that” type of answer). However, I don’t blame them for this, the people I asked were friends and family who don’t partake in this type of learning, for their careers or other wise. I could’ve asked others who did have more knowledge on this, but I feel like they don’t know me well enough to provide accurate information, or they wouldn’t feel comfortable.

Why do you think we, a programming school, are spending so much time focusing on core learning in a web development bootcamp course?


Web development is essentially creating a product or tool to be used by people. If you don’t understand how people think, how are you supposed to effectively create a tool for someone to understand and use? Beyond that, coding is often a transport. Creating code is a big job, and usually requires a team. If you can’t work with others effectively, this will halt progress on projects and cause more conflicts and unnecessary stress.

what is your long term goal and/or career pathway?


I’d like to design small business web platforms that are accessible for the ordinary person, easy to understand and helpful. Whether this means going to work in a team that provides this kind of system already, or attempting to create my own, time will tell.

What do you think your biggest strengths and limitations will be in Bootcamp?


think my biggest strengths will be around my work ethic. When I get started, I don’t like to stop until I’m done and I put in the hours. However, I do quite usually run the risk of not taking breaks and when I do, I find it hard to start up again. I think I’ll effectively use time-boxing to try help with this.

What do you think your biggest non-technical challenge at Bootcamp will be?


I think I may struggle to work with a team, just because all of it will be online. I work better with people when I can effectively get to know them and I feel like doing this online may be a barrier.

What non-technical skills - human skills - would you like to see developed in yourself while at EDA?


I would like to learn more about working in group projects and working alongside others, as I haven’t had as much chance to do this as I would like in previous roles. So I’ve touched on it a little, but it was never anything substantial.

What are your expectations of yourself on Bootcamp


I’m expecting it to be really tough, but equally rewarding. In terms of reaching out for help, it can be difficult with this being online, but I’ll definitely make a conscious effort to reach out to my peers and tutors when need be. What this will look like is first using my problem solving solutions before seeking help, first rely on my peers (as they may be struggling as well) and then reach out to a tutor. I have to make sure not to be too reliant, but also not allow myself to just get stuck on something and be unable to move forward.
